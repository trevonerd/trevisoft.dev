# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Trevisoft serves people discovering Marco Trevisani's tools, experiments, and side projects, plus HR and recruiting teams reviewing Marco after receiving a CV. Tool-seekers need to understand quickly what each project does and where to open it. HR visitors need a credible, memorable snapshot of range, craft, taste, and shipping habit without digging through every repository.

## Product Purpose

Trevisoft is the public home for Marco's independent software work: web apps, browser extensions, game tools, media utilities, creative generators, and whatever ships next. The site should make the project collection feel alive and useful, while giving visitors enough context to trust the maker behind it. Success means a visitor can scan the work, open the right tool, and leave with a clear impression: Marco ships practical, polished, curious software.

## Brand Personality

Curious, precise, playful. The voice should feel technical but human, independent but not amateur, and quietly proud without becoming corporate. The moustache mark and synth-lab atmosphere are part of the identity, but they should feel calibrated rather than loud.

## Anti-references

Trevisoft should not feel like an overproduced neon arcade, a generic AI landing page, a noisy portfolio template, a cramped card wall, or a decorative effects demo where particles and glow compete with the projects. Avoid wrong spacing rhythms, excessive visual effects, HR-unfriendly inside jokes as the main read, and anything that makes the page harder to scan.

## Design Principles

1. Show the tools first. Project utility, status, and actions should be obvious before decoration registers.
2. Earn the weirdness. Personality works best as a precise accent, not as a blanket treatment over every surface.
3. Stay CV-ready. The site can be playful, but it must also signal judgment, accessibility, and real shipped work.
4. Calm the surface, keep the pulse. Preserve the synth identity while reducing visual noise, glow stacking, and spacing errors.
5. Make every project scannable. Cards, tags, and actions should help visitors compare projects quickly without reading every sentence.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Use WCAG AA as the baseline for contrast, keyboard access, and focus visibility. Respect reduced-motion preferences, avoid relying on color alone for project meaning, and keep the easter egg nonblocking. The page should work for quick HR review, mobile browsing, and users who skim with assistive technology.
